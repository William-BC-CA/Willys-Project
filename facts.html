1. The First Electric Cars were actually first made in around the 19-20th century Electric cars have actually existed long before than you've expected. It has existed way longer than today's Tesla Motors or even the General Motors EV1 of the late 1990s. During the 1830s, Robert Anderson from Scotland, whose motorized carriage was built sometime between 1832 and '39. Batteries consisted of galvanic cells which were not yet rechargeable, which made to look more of a parlor trick. However, Robert Davidson of Aberdeen, built a prototype electric locomotive back in 1837. A bigger and better version which was later demonstrated in 1841, was able to reach about 2.4 kilometers at 6.4 km/h towing about six tons of cargo. Apparently, it would run to the point where it needed new batteries. The performance was however so alarmed that railway workers (having seen it as a threat) decided to destroy Davidson's devil machine, which he'd later called Galvani. Car And Driver

The new car smell is produced by over 50 volatile organic compounds You heard it right, the new car smell is actually made up of a mix of chemicals, which are contained in the various car interior features. These features can release volatile organic compounds, which produce the familiar smell. Some have rumored them to be toxic. Over the years, numerous people have wondered whether or not the chemicals that cause the new car smell are toxic and hazardous to human health.
From an American standpoint, "these chemicals can be harmful when inhaled or ingested and may lead to severe health impacts such as birth defects, learning disabilities and cancer. Since the average American spends more than 1.5 hours in a car every day, toxic chemical exposure inside vehicles is becoming a major source of potential indoor air pollution." This isn't really a major problem but rather something that can be removed by simply buying a used car as the odor would start to fade dramatically over time.		 Fact: Fred Beans
Information: First Time Driver
